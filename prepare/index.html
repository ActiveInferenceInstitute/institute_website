Project Preparation is how an idea becomes an active project at the Active Inference Institute. It guides a proposal through scoping and readiness review so the work can launch within the EduActive education unit or the ReInference research unit. The pathway is open and participatory: anyone can understand how projects come to life, and how preparation connects to funding discovery and the public projects registry. The detailed steps, proposal templates, and readiness checklists live on the preparation hub and are being migrated onto this site.

From idea to active project

Project Preparation is the structured onboarding pathway that moves an initial idea through proposal, scoping, and readiness review until it is ready to operate as an active project. The pathway helps clarify what a project intends to do, which Institute unit it belongs in, and what is needed before it begins, turning loosely held intentions into something the community can support and sustain. As a participatory, open-science nonprofit guided by the motto Act. Infer. Serve., the Institute keeps this pathway transparent so that contributors at any stage can see the route from a first idea to a working project listed alongside everything else under way.

Where prepared projects live: EduActive and ReInference

Prepared projects launch within one of the Institute's two program units. The EduActive unit hosts education-oriented activities such as courses, learning resources, and community programs, while the ReInference unit hosts research-oriented work such as studies, software, and applied investigation. Both operate alongside the Administrative unit under the Institute's 501(c)(3) nonprofit governance. A central part of preparation is determining which unit best fits a project's aims, so that once it becomes active it connects to the right facilitation, community, and resources.

The preparation arc: proposal, scoping, readiness

Preparation typically progresses through a few connected stages. A proposal describes the idea: what the activity is, who would be involved, and why it matters. Scoping then sharpens that description into a workable shape, defining boundaries, expected outputs, and the unit it belongs in. Readiness review checks that the pieces needed to begin are in place before the project is treated as active. The specific stages, the forms each takes, and the criteria that mark a project as ready are described in detail on the preparation hub, which the pathway references as a project moves through onboarding.

Funding discovery and resources

Many projects need resources to operate, and preparation is the right moment to consider how a project might be supported. The Institute's grants and funding pages describe the pathways available for discovering and pursuing financial support, and preparation connects directly to them so that scoping and funding can be considered together rather than in isolation. Describing the kind of resources a project requires during scoping makes it easier to match an idea to the funding routes that fit it.

Open and participatory by design

Because the Institute is participatory and open by design, the preparation pathway is meant to be approachable rather than gated. Contributors do not need an existing affiliation to begin shaping an idea; the pathway exists precisely to help newcomers and established participants alike move work toward an active footing. The broader activities, structure, and ways to get involved across the Institute provide the surrounding context, so a prepared project arrives already connected to the community and governance that will sustain it.
